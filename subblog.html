An Unexpected Journey

Into Software Development!

To turn over a new leaf

As a civil engineer, I am a civil engineer, not a software developer. Or at least that’s what I thought. This is the story of how I followed my passion of becoming a software developer.

The beginning

It all began on a fine thursday afternoon. It was 2pm, the sky was blue, the streets were empty and I was out on my daily walk, breathing in the fresh, covid-free air.

I love my walks. I find walking to be one of the rocks in my life. Walks center me and bring me back to my base. They’re a time where I can breathe, relax, and consider, and evaluate my thoughts and feelings. And on this calm day, birds chirping atop their tall trees, a day where I would typically have felt at peace, energised, and happy, I was instead feeling distracted and dissatisfied; something in my life wasn’t as it should be.

Employment nowadays, is a fundamental and time intensive part of life. A few years ago, I fostered a very close bond with a beautiful puppy (he wasn’t a puppy but all dogs are puppies in my eyes) named Louie. Louie’s dad was the to-be CEO of the engineering consultancy I have been employed by for the last few years. I also tutored his daughter in maths but a dog’s approval is a massive credibility statement. I enjoy my work in civil engineering as I love to problem solve. But I also had to acknowledge this feeling in my gut that was drawing me towards software development. And in the clarity of the fresh air on this wonderful day, I realised as silly as it sounds, that I didn’t have to have a software development qualification or be a software developer by trade to know how to develop software. Anyone can do it! And so I decided that it was time to turn a new leaf, and begin my unexpected journey into the digital world.

My journey started from the bottom. After deciding upon my new pursuit, I quickly realised how little I knew about where to start without re-enrolling in a computer science qualification at university. I needed wisdom from the inside, and so I called upon my already qualified friends for some insight - what do I need to do to make myself a competent software developer?

The three people I contacted all offered some excellent suggestions and with a bit of thought and extra research, I have constructed a pathway to my goal of becoming a software developer. This blog will detail the steps I have taken and the steps I am yet to take on my unexpected journey.

The very first step

In my eyes, the main fundamental to becoming a software developer (software dev) is learning your first language. Languages are the interaction between the computer and the individual and there are languages serving all types of purposes and utilities. Some are more common than others, notable names being Python, JavaScript, C (and it’s descendants C++, C# etc…), HTML, CSS, Java, PHP, and others. My friend recommended I begin learning JavaScript and he directed me to a brilliant and free learning platform - FreeCodeCamp.org. He said I should complete their JavaScript Algorithms and Data Structures Certification, a 300hour course which took me approximately 1 month, spending between 1-6 hours per day working through their interactive courses.

FreeCodeCamp.org was the perfect place to start for me because their JavaScript program eases the user into the development environment, providing the user with clear instructions and explanations of all the topics, each introduced at a very natural progression rate. The platform gives the user a series of tasks, explanations, and examples and then adjacent to this information, there is a built in integrated development environment (IDE) in which you can script your solutions and run them against the pre-existing test queries.

I feel that this course is the perfect place to begin because it provides the user with their first tier of credibility; the user can now interact with the software development world. The user has their first tool in their tool kit to start building their profile and portfolio as a software developer, even being able to complete coding exams is a great feeling! The additional benefit of selecting JavaScript as a first language is that it can be used for full-stack development.

- a brief interlude as ‘full-stack’ was a term whose meaning eluded me for a while. In development, there is front-end development, which involves the creation and development of the client side interface or user experience. Conversely, there is back-end development which communicates front-end information and inputs to databases where data is organised and stored. Full-stack development is when the developer develops both front-end and back-end systems. -

Congratulations! You can now write code. If you enjoyed your experience with FreeCodeCamp.org, you can also buy their cool hoodies to support their platform and also look cool with some hip swag.

With your flash, new certification, it’s my opinion that you are now ready to download and set up your personal development environment so that you can script your own code. This step had me absolutely duped if I'm completely honest. Having had the luxury of FreeCodeCamp’s built-in coding environment, I genuinely had no idea where to write my own code. I tried writing it on some paper but I couldn’t find a run button so that didn’t work. But this is where the IDE comes in handy. IDE’s aren’t required for coding but they make life very easy. They allow you to script, run, and debug your code all inside one application. There are a plethora of different environments but my personal favorite for beginners is Visual Studio Code. It’s incredibly easy to set up and after adding extensions relevant to the languages you plan on using, you can install the Power Mode extension which leaves a flaming trail as you blaze along the screen with you 100 wpm scripting speed.

Looking cool on the interwebs

When trying to catch large bears, you first have to go through the long wavy grass (learning your first language). Naturally, after traversing the long grass, you have to splash through the deep cold river, you can’t go over it and you can’t go under it. This is obviously representative of developing your professional image (on LinkedIn). It’s good to list your expertise and state your intentions. Spend time relating your current work experiences to software development, speaking to your history of problem solving, project management, team work, and communication.

I also recently discovered that LinkedIn offers a badge certification system to your skills as well. The skills section is an important part of your profile as it denotes your proficiencies, which can now be accredited with LinkedIn badges. My first badge was the JavaScript badge. If you complete LinkedIn’s exam on a particular skill (they’re fairly challenging and you only get a limited number of opportunities to attempt it every period of time), and score in the top 30% of all applicants, then you can have LinkedIn’s stamp of approval next to the skill listed on your profile! I love badges and so I loved hearing this.

Finally, and this is entirely my opinion, choose a profile picture that you feel best represents you. I feel that professional images are boring and unauthentic but a picture of you with a big grin across your face out on your favorite hike, or doing something else that feels characteristic of you, gives people a personable and friendly impression of you :)

Pro tip - make your website really detailed and keep your CV to one tidy page!

Communities

Another great way to gain experience, familiarise yourself with the industry, and just generally enhance your experience as a software developer is to join related communities. I personally enjoy social networking and having the opportunity to engage with others and learn from their experience is a great way to feel enthused about the journey ahead. Speaking with individuals involved in the subsectors of software development that most excite you is another great way to guide your development experience and minimise the time you spend feeling lost along the way.

The first community I found was called Design Buddies. Design Buddies host numerous learning and socialising events that cater to all types of developers, and the community feels very inviting. I have also found that most universities have developer clubs that are easy to get involved with too!

Creating the famed portfolio

Now it’s time for a bit of a project. You’ve become a competent user of your first language, and your public image is aligned with your developer goals, so now you’re more than ready to branch out and build your own website.

Why build a website, you might ask. You’ve already got a CV and a neat looking LinkedIn page, surely that’s sufficient? If you’re asking these questions, you’re not wrong, but for me, building a website wasn’t about developing my public image; it was an exciting, learning opportunity where I could gain some knowledge on new languages, HTML and CSS, and also the thought of having a page, with my own URL that I could link people to, and completely personalise, was super exciting. And as much as this experience doesn’t have to be purposed with the sake of finding employment, it really will hike your credibility score well over 9000. Especially if it’s customised to represent you. New to the development world, where you probably don’t have any apps on the app store, or your own steam game, it’s important to convey your personality. Anyone can be taught to script code and make commits to GitHub, but not everyone is fun, considerate, and a capable communicator, or even a team player, leader, or innovator.

Git, GitHub, Agile - the alien words

By now, I had heard the term Git or GitHub come up all over the place more than 1000 times over. I’d also heard terms such as Scrum and Agile pop up too. These soft-knowledge topics and skills are great to familiarise yourself with as not only will they help enhance your development lifestyle but they’re also integral to company mechanics and coding collectively.

Git and GitHub refer to a collaboration network where people regularly upload their code to a cloud database. More specifically, people use Git to upload to GitHub, and within GitHub, there are repositories (folders) which contain all versions of all uploaded projects. People are able to use Git to pull information and project work from GitHub, work on it, and push commits (essentially modifications with descriptions) back to GitHub, where the changes are clearly visible. This system allows everyone to collaborate on code and even allows the user to store their own code in a neat, cohesive, and efficient system.

Terms like Agile are more representative of practices as a coder. Wikipedia defines it as “discovering requirements and developing solutions through the collaborative effort of self-organizing and cross-functional teams and their customer(s)/end user(s)”. It’s a workplace way of working to optimise communication, individual productivity, and collective success as a team, achieving a shared outcome.

Scrum is a common Agile methodology / framework, providing iterative and incremental guidance along the lifespan of a project. The scrum framework specifically defines the organisational layout across two week periods, detailing how progress is tracked and key objectives of future work. Scrum is an implementation of Agile practices (but there are numerous flavours of implementation).

There is far more information regarding all of these topics available than what I’ve described, but at least now you might understand what the words represent :)
